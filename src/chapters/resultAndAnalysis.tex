\chapter{RESULTS AND ANALYSIS}

This chapter presents the evaluation of the baseline forecasting models described in
Chapter 5, using the chronologically split test set and the regression metrics defined
in Section 4.5.1 (MAE, RMSE, and $R^2$). Results are reported both as an aggregate
across all 49 monitoring stations and, where the aggregate is misleading, on a
per-station basis. The chapter also documents a data-quality investigation that was
carried out to explain the station-level results.

\section{Baseline Model Comparison}

Two baseline models were evaluated: a persistence baseline, and a linear regression
model, the latter tested across three feature configurations to isolate the effect of
regularization and feature pruning (see Section 5.2.2). Table~\ref{tab:baseline_model_comparison}
summarizes the average test-set performance for each configuration, and
Figure~\ref{fig:model_comparison} presents the same comparison visually.

        \hline
        \textbf{Model} & \textbf{MAE} & \textbf{RMSE} & \textbf{$R^2$} \\

\begin{figure}[H]

The plain OLS configuration performs far worse than even the persistence baseline
($R^2 = -2.982$), with the final Ridge configuration recovering to $R^2 = 0.448$. The
final Ridge model still remains slightly below the persistence baseline, which is
expected at this stage: persistence is a strong baseline for one-hour-ahead PM$_{2.5}$
forecasting since pollution levels change little hour to hour, and a linear model using
only a single station's own history has limited additional signal to extract. The
wind-aware graph structure (Section 5.3) is intended to supply the additional spatial
signal -- from neighboring, upwind stations -- needed to meaningfully exceed the
persistence baseline. The large gap between the OLS and Ridge configurations in
Table~\ref{tab:baseline_model_comparison}, however, is too large to be explained by
persistence alone, and was investigated further.

\section{Impact of Regularization on Model Generalization}

The unusually poor OLS result prompted a feature correlation analysis, which revealed
severe multicollinearity among the engineered PM$_{2.5}$-derived features (e.g., a
Pearson correlation of 0.968 between \texttt{lag\_1} and \texttt{rolling\_mean\_3}). With
input columns this collinear, the OLS design matrix for several stations had a
condition number on the order of $10^{16}$--$10^{18}$ (a well-conditioned matrix is
typically $10^2$--$10^4$), causing unstable, unbounded coefficient estimates.
Table~\ref{tab:regularization_generalization_impact} summarizes the effect of
addressing this through regularization and feature pruning, and
Figure~\ref{fig:ols_vs_ridge} illustrates the effect directly on one representative
station.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \small
    \caption{Impact of Regularization on Model Generalization}
    \label{tab:regularization_generalization_impact}
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
    \begin{tabularx}{0.94\linewidth}{|X|X|}
        \hline
        \textbf{Configuration} & \textbf{Effect} \\
        \hline
        OLS, all features & Severe overfitting to multicollinear inputs; unstable, unbounded predictions on several stations; mean $R^2 = -2.98$ \\
        \hline
        Ridge ($\alpha=10$), all features & Coefficient instability controlled; mean $R^2 = 0.415$ \\
        \hline
        Ridge ($\alpha=10$), pruned features & Redundant inputs removed in addition to regularization; mean $R^2 = 0.448$, median $R^2 = 0.560$ \\
        \hline

\end{table}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering

Figure~\ref{fig:ols_vs_ridge} shows actual-versus-predicted PM$_{2.5}$ on the test set
for a station where the OLS model produced physically implausible predictions.
Switching to Ridge regression brings predictions back within a realistic range, and
pruning the redundant lag/rolling features (Section 5.2.2) improves the mean $R^2$
further, from 0.415 to 0.448. This confirms that the OLS failure was a numerical
instability caused by feature redundancy, rather than a flaw in the underlying linear
modeling approach.

\section{Station-wise Performance and Outlier Analysis}

The aggregate Ridge result reported in Table~\ref{tab:baseline_model_comparison} is a
mean across 49 stations, which can obscure whether performance is broadly consistent or
driven by a small number of poorly performing stations. Per-station results, shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:station_r2}, indicate a wide but largely healthy spread:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Median $R^2$ across 49 stations: \textbf{0.560} (higher than the mean of 0.448, since the mean is pulled down by a small number of underperforming stations)
    \item 47 of 49 stations achieve a positive $R^2$, most in the 0.3--0.9 range
    \item Best-performing stations ($R^2 > 0.85$): Dabali, Sundarighat, Tarakeswor, Dhathutole
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{src/images/figures/fig_station_r2.png}
    \caption{Station-wise $R^2$ performance}
    \label{fig:station_r2}
\end{figure}

One station, \textbf{Gothatar}, remains a clear outlier ($R^2 = -3.32$, MAE = 16.5) in
Figure~\ref{fig:station_r2} despite a comparatively large training set (1,620 rows) --
traced in Section~\ref{sec:timestamp_continuity} to a large data gap in its history. Two
stations with short monitoring histories -- \textbf{Ramkot} (206 total rows) and
\textbf{Sifal} (116 total rows) -- were initially suspected of underperforming due to
insufficient training data; after feature pruning, both achieved reasonable $R^2$ (0.40
and 0.67 respectively), indicating that multicollinearity, not data volume, was the
dominant issue for most stations. Gothatar's outlier status, however, remained
unexplained by the modeling fixes alone, which motivated a direct investigation of its
underlying data quality.

\section{Timestamp Continuity Findings}
\label{sec:timestamp_continuity}

To investigate the Gothatar outlier and confirm overall data reliability, timestamp
continuity was validated across all 49 prepared station files, checking whether
consecutive readings are spaced exactly one hour apart (Section 5.1.5). Overall data
quality was high: every station exceeded 93\% valid (exactly one-hour) gaps between
consecutive readings, with most stations at 98--99\%, as shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:timestamp_validity}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{src/images/figures/fig_timestamp_validity.png}
    \caption{Timestamp validity by station}
    \label{fig:timestamp_validity}
\end{figure}

Despite this generally high data quality, three stations stood out on closer inspection:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Gothatar} shows a single gap of approximately 2,906 hours (\textasciitilde{}121 days) despite an otherwise 98.9\% valid-gap rate -- the most likely explanation for its poor model performance, since it plausibly falls near the train/test split boundary and creates a distribution mismatch.
    \item \textbf{Ramkot} has a 1,099-hour (\textasciitilde{}46-day) gap within an already short 206-row history.
    \item \textbf{Sifal} has zero internal gaps (100\% valid hourly spacing) but only 116 total rows -- this station simply began monitoring recently, rather than suffering data-quality issues.
\end{itemize}

This confirms that Gothatar's poor performance is a genuine data limitation rather than
a modeling error, and distinguishes it from Ramkot and Sifal, whose short histories are
a separate, unrelated limitation.

\section{Summary of Key Findings}

\begin{itemize}
    \item The persistence baseline is a genuinely strong reference point ($R^2$ = 0.708) for one-hour-ahead PM$_{2.5}$ forecasting due to the high hour-to-hour autocorrelation of pollution levels.
    \item An initial OLS linear regression failed catastrophically ($R^2$ = -2.98) due to severe multicollinearity among engineered lag/rolling PM$_{2.5}$ features, not due to a flaw in the underlying modeling approach.
    \item Switching to Ridge regression and pruning redundant features recovered a stable, reasonable baseline ($R^2$ = 0.448 mean / 0.560 median), though it does not yet exceed persistence -- expected at this stage, since a per-station linear model cannot access cross-station wind-driven information.
    \item Data-quality investigation (timestamp continuity validation) explained nearly all station-level outliers, distinguishing genuine data limitations (Gothatar's gap, Ramkot's and Sifal's short histories) from modeling errors.
    \item These findings directly motivate the remaining work: the wind-aware graph and GAT-GRU model are expected to supply the additional cross-station signal needed to exceed the persistence baseline.
\end{itemize}
